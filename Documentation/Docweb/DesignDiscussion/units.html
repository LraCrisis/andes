Units in Andes2 Design

Joel

This is a list of questions relevant to the treatment of physical units in the Andes2 system.

It is generally agreed that proper treatment of units in Andes lies somewhere between desirable and essential. The algebra system has been designed to be able to carry unit checking, and work is in progress on transferring the necessary information. Some of the issues are

  • Do we need to incorporate units nontrivially in Andes?

    • Physical quantities generally have dimensions --- a speed can be 8 m/s or 8 miles/hr but it is meaningless to say the speed is 8. Such language lost a Martial probe costing hundreds of millions of dollars. Andes1 had the dimensions of quantities in the knowledge base but made no use of them other than knowing what to ignore in a student's answer. I believe we are now agreed that Andes2 should require correct units. Here is some of the discussion that led to that (just what I could dig up - there are undoubtedly many missing pieces)

      I believe the upshot of this discussion is that units should be expressed in book (or canonical) form kg.m/s^2 in a defprob and in any subsequent expressions that a physicist might need to examine, but that it be converted to a lisp form (* kg (/ m (^ s 2))) before being presented to the algebra system. [Actually the last comment has been reconsidered, and we are now planning to store all units in "book form", eg. kg.m/s^2] [Note: this probably means that the probname.Eqn file has lisp form, even though a physicist might examine it.]
  • How units are represented internally in the algebra system

    • The algebra system is designed to express all quantities in SI units with given powers of meters [m], kilograms [kg], seconds [s], Coulombs [C], and degrees Kelvin [K]. However, there are unresolved issues which will need subtle consideration:
    • How are angles expressed? Currently all angles (without units) are interpreted as in degrees, even though this is highly non-standard. Angular velocities and accelerations, however, are expressed in rad/sec, which will certainly give problems once we consider waves and want to evaluate cos(omega t). Furthermore, the rules for dropping rad are irreversible - while it is easy to recognize that in v = omega r if omega = 10 rad/s and r = 2 m, v is 20 m/s and not 20 m rad/s, it is less easy to rcognize that, given v and r, omega is not 10/s.
    • What non-standard units are accepted, and do we simply convert on input and give all answers in MKS, or convert at output as well, or not convert. See Exkt1a, Exkt9a.
    • Even the standard units have many abbreviations - they are not always expressed in terms of the fundamental units. Here is a list of SI units (not necessarily complete)

      • m, kg, s, N, J, K, C, V, A, T, ohm, Wb, Hz, Pa, F, H, W
    • and there are standard prefix modifiers, of which we should recognize

      • a, f, p, n, mu, m, c, k or K, M, G, T, P, E , from which I have dropped the little used d, h, da
    • There are a set of nearly essential alternates to SI:

      • eV, MeV, c, u, Angstrom, atm, f (fermi), barn, cal, ^oC, lightyear, liter,
      and also English and other archaic units:
      Btu, ^oF, ft, in, hr, min, hp, lb, day, yr, yd, mi, gal, oz, slug, bar, Kwh
      and of course physicists sometimes slip into cgs units:
      CGS: G (gauss), g gram, dyne, erg
