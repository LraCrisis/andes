Pseudo errors: Cases that Andes1 marked incorrect which Andes2 should mark correct

(as of 3/20/01)

AMM at 5-47

A car can go from rest to 22.4 m/s in 8.20 seconds.  Assuming the acceleration is constant, how long will it take to speed up from 13.4 m/s to 18.9 m/s?  The student has solved the problem correctly and efficiently, and entered the answer correctly.  The student has also drawn the displacement of the car from T0 to T1, and Andes 1 marks it wrong.  Andes2 should mark it correct, because it is part of a less efficient solution.

bryan at 20-14

A block is launched from a spring at T0, flies off the spring at T1 and reaches the apex of its trajectory at T2.  The sought quantity is the height, but the student's first action is to draw the displacement of the block from T0 to T2.  The student should have defined a variable for the height of the block at T2 instead.  This is a rather arbitrary convention of Andes that should just be told to the student (see discussion).

The error handler is: The student defines a displacement of ?body from ?t0 to ?t1, and that quantity is not used in the problem, but the height of ?body at time ?t1 is used, then point/teach/state that for problems where gravitational potential energy is used, Andes expresses it in terms of a height quantity rather than a displacement.

This hint sequence gives away the fact that the relevant PSM is conservation of energy.  We could do better, but if we change the workbench as per the discussion, then this error can't occur anymore.

bush2000 at 17-12

A bullet leaves the gun at T0, hits the gong  at T1 and the sound reaches the shooter at T2.  The student defines a variable for the magnitude of the displacement of the sound from T1 to T2.  This is almost correct, but Andes wants the student to define a distance instead of a displacement.  Same discussion as before: maybe one spatial measurement tool should be used instead of several.  Otherwise, we need to trap this error and give a hint sequence that teaches when to use which tool.

Ingpole at 8-35

If a roller coaster tops a 20 m hill moving at a speed of 3 m/s, then what is its speed at the bottom of the hill?  The student has defined a variable for the height of the hill when it should be the height of the coaster.

This is a non-obvious convention of Andes: Even when the problem specifies a spatial measurement for a hill, inclined plane or some other object that is not a body, the principles use spatial measures that involve only the bodies.  Although we could ask authors not to include non-body objects in the menus for spatial measures, that would be an easy request to overlook.  We could also teach the convention to students, but they have enough to learn without that.  Thus, it is better to catch this "error" and handle it by mapping the student's quantity into the equivalent correct quantity.  To do that, we need some new propositions in the problem representation, namely, same-position(object1, time1, object2, time2).  These propositions are used only by this error handler.

The error handler is: "If the student has drawn a spatial measure from ?body1 at ?time1 to ?body2 at ?time2, and that spatial measure is not used in the solution, but there is a spatial measure in the solution from ?bodyA at ?timeA to ?bodyB at ?timeB, and
(or (and (equal ?body1 ?bodyA)
         (equal ?time1 ?timeA))
    (and (same-position ?body1, ?time-includes1, ?bodyA, time-includesA)
         (tinside ?time1 ?time-includes1)
         (tinside ?timeA ?time-includesA)))
and
(or (and (equal ?body2 ?bodyB)
         (equal ?time2 ?timeB))
    (and (same-position ?body2, ?time-includes2, ?bodyB, time-includesB)
         (tinside ?time2 ?time-includes2)
         (tinside ?timeB ?time-includesB)),
then put an entry in the symbol table that maps the student's spatial measure onto the solution's spatial measure."
