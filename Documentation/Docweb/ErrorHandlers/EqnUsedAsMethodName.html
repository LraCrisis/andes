Equations used as method names

(as of 3/27/01)

Students sometimes use equations such as F=ma, W=mg, U=mgh, etc. as names for methods.  Usually this just causes undefined variable or illegal syntax errors (implicit multiplication is illegal, so F=ma won't parse right but F=m*a will).  However, sometimes the student has all the variables defined, in which case we need a bug to detect what the student is trying to do.  Basically, we need one bug per equation, but we can add them as we see them.  Here's the bug for F=m*a:

If the student entry is an equation

  • that has the form <force> = <mass>*<acceleration> or <mass>*<acceleration>=<force> where the vector quantities are either magnitudes or components, and <force> can be either the net force or any individual force,
then the hint sequence is
  • a trainning card hint: "Are you writing F=m*a just to show that you intend to apply Newton's law?  Newton's law is indeed the right approach to solving this problem.  However, Andes interprets all your equations as specific statements about the problem.  In particular, it interprets "f=m*a" in terms of the variables f, m and a that you have defined for this problem.  In this case, the definitions of at least one of those variables makes this equation inappropriate for the problem.  

    If you just want to provide an explanation to the reader, then put a semi-colon (;) before it.  Andes ignores everything in the box after the semi-colon.   For instance, if you want to say that you were going to apply Newton's law, then you could just but "; F=ma" in the equation box (without the double quotes, of course.).  This will stay black instead of turning red or green, because Andes doesn't read anything after the semi-colon.  However, whoever reads the printed copy of your work will see it and know that your subsequent equations will be an application of Newton's law."
A KCD version of this would treat the first question "Are you writing F=m*a just to show that you intend to apply Newton's law?" as a real question.

If a person of known mass is hanging from a bridge by a bungee cord, what is the tension in the cord?  The student defined f, m and a variables (no FBD, though) then entered “f=m*a” as the first equation, where f is defined as the tension force, not the net force.
  • IH first points out the discrepancy between tension force and net force, then asks if a has any particular value in this problem.
  • RO doesn’t identify a correct equation but questions ‘f’ and asks for a FBD.
  • Andes1 says, “Maybe you should multiply by ‘G’ rather than by ‘a’ in the right side of the equation.”
  • KVL: Since this is a first-law problem, the <net-force>=m*a equation is not needed, and it is unlikely that she solved the whole problem in her head and was trying the write f=m*g, which is the answer. The student is probably using "f=m*a" as a name for Newton's law, rather than a real equation.  Students are often taught to provide such comments as part of their homework solutions.  The best advice is probably to address this misunderstanding of how to use equations in the equation window.
Andes could find analyses for this equation by substituting the net force for f or by substituting g for a.  That would lead to advice similar to IH/RO and Andes1.  However, let's use this error handler: "If the student's entry is literally 'f=m*a' or 'm*a=f', and the solution uses Newton's first or second law, then say, 'Are you writing F=m*a just to show that you intend to apply Newton's law?  Newton's law is indeed the right approach to solving this problem.  However, Andes interprets all your equations as specific statements about the problem.  In particular, it interprets "f=m*a" in terms of the variables f, m and a that you have defined for this problem.  In this case, the definitions of at least one of those variables makes this equation inappropriate for the problem.  If you just want to provide an explanation to the reader, then put a semi-colon (;) before it.  Andes ignores everything in the box after the semi-colon."  This should be conveyed with a training card instead of a hint.
