Zero net force when speeding up

(as of 3/27/01)

If the student entry is a net force

  • that has zero magnitude
  • and the body's motion is straight and speeding up in direction ?dir
  • and making the net force non-zero in direction ?dir yields a correct vector
then the hint sequence is
  • (hint string (point) "Is the net force zero even though the body is moving in a straight line and speeding up?")
  • (hint string (teach) "Whenever a body is moving in a straight line and speeding up, it is accelerating.  Whenever a body is accelerating, there is a net force acting on it in the same direction as the acceleration.  This follows directly from Newton's second law, F=m*a.")
  • (hint string (bottom-out) "Because the body is speeding up in direction ~a, it is accelerating in direction ~a, so you should change the net force so that it is non-zero and in direction ~a." ?dir ?dir ?dir)
The hints must be worded so that they make sense even if the student has drawn the acceleration correctly or incorrectly.

A KCD version of the teaching hint would go like this one:  "Is ?body moving in a straight line and speeding up at ?time?" (Ideal Student Answer: Yes) "Does ?body have a non-zero acceleration? (Ideal Student Answer: Yes)  "If a body has a non-zero acceleration, is the net force on it zero or non-zero?"  (Ideal student answer: zero) "But you have drawn a zero-length net force vector.  You should change that."

Clearly, we need similar bugs for the the slowing down case and for the circular motion case.  We also need similar handlers for errors where the student has drawn a zero acceleration in for an object that is speeding up, slowing down or moving in a circle.

Karlton-Holston at 35-57

A car starts from rest at T0 and rolls down a frictionless 20 m driveway, smashing into the garage door at T1.  The student draws a zero-length net force on the car, but the actual net force is non-zero.  Andes2 interprets the error as an attempt to draw the non-zero net force (one mismatch: magnitude).  SG analysis indicates that the operator application is not premature and not efficient.  However, it is not an inefficiency that Andes should point out (see discussion), basically because it is more important to get the physics right than to learn the most efficient procedure.
