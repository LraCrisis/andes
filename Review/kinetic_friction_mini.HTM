Friction Force

When an object contacts another surface and either tries to move or actually moves across it, the surface exerts a friction force on the object.

 This friction force is parallel to the surface and opposes the motion of the object relative to the surface.

 When the object actually moves across the surface, the force is called kinetic friction force.

 Friction is caused by the microscopic bonding and interlocking of the tiny bulges and indentations of one surface with another.

One way of visualizing this is shown in the animation below. In it, the moving object is represented by the purple rectangle. When it moves across the gray surface, the surface's tiny dents are bent downward. In reaction, the bent stationary dents push back on moving object's dents in the upward direction. This is the kinetic friction force that surface applies to the moving object.  Notice that the friction force is in the upward direction, that is, in the direction opposite the object's motion.  The friction force is always parallel to the surface and in the direction opposite the object's motion.
